\begin{abstract}
Hybrid quantum-classical architectures are easy to overclaim on small biomedical benchmarks, where
strong classical baselines and low statistical power can make minor differences look decisive. We
revisit a lightweight teacher-student pipeline with PCA compression, a compact classical student, a
4-qubit parameterized quantum layer, and knowledge distillation (KD) for biomedical tabular
classification. We rerun the main Breast Cancer Wisconsin Diagnostic (WDBC) comparison with
10-fold cross-validation, retain the original WDBC ablations, and add companion Pima Diabetes and
Heart Statlog benchmarks plus pilot cross-dataset placement checks.

On WDBC, the tail-placement Hybrid-KD model matches but does not reliably outperform the classical
student (AUC $0.9926 \pm 0.0095$ vs.\ $0.9947 \pm 0.0081$), and the hybrid cost remains in the
hundreds of seconds per fold rather than sub-second. The placement result also needs a narrower
interpretation than our original draft: front insertion is fragile in this implementation because
direct PCA-to-qubit mapping induces heavy \texttt{Tanh} saturation and removes the learnable
pre-quantum projection, whereas middle and tail remain viable. Pima and Heart show only modest,
statistically uncertain hybrid gains under much higher simulation cost.

We therefore present the study as a pilot ablation of placement and input conditioning, not as
definitive evidence that lightweight hybrid models outperform compact classical baselines.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Quantum machine learning (QML) continues to attract interest as a possible route toward practical
learning systems on near-term quantum hardware. In practice, however, large quantum circuits remain
hard to optimize and expensive to simulate, especially in data regimes where strong classical baselines
are already available~\cite{cerezo2021vqa}. Hybrid quantum-classical (HQC) models therefore remain the
most realistic design pattern: a classical front-end performs feature compression, while a small
parameterized quantum circuit (PQC) acts as a learnable nonlinear block~\cite{schuld2021machine}.

Biomedical tabular classification is a useful setting for testing this idea. Such datasets are often
small, feature-limited after preprocessing, and measured under strict performance-cost constraints.
A compact HQC model may therefore provide insight into how and where a quantum block can be inserted,
even if no quantum advantage is expected. At the same time, knowledge distillation (KD) offers a
natural mechanism for transferring supervision from a larger classical teacher to a smaller classical
or hybrid student~\cite{kd}.

This paper asks three concrete questions on the WDBC benchmark:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Does KD help a hybrid quantum-classical student, and how does the KD weight $\alpha$ affect the
trade-off between AUC and F1/MCC?
\item Where should the quantum layer be placed inside the student network?
\item Is the hybrid student worth its simulation cost relative to a lightweight classical student,
considering both training and inference time?
\end{enumerate}

Our contributions are as follows. First, we revisit the main classical-versus-hybrid comparison on
WDBC with a 10-fold robustness check and explicit uncertainty reporting rather than relying only on
5-fold mean$\pm$std summaries. Second, we retain the WDBC ablations on placement, PCA dimension, and
KD weight $\alpha$ while extending the study with companion Pima Diabetes and Heart Statlog
benchmarks. Third, we add pilot cross-dataset placement checks on Heart and Pima to test whether the
front/middle/tail pattern is specific to WDBC. Fourth, we provide a circuit diagram, discuss the
limited expressibility of the 4-qubit 1-layer ansatz, and explicitly frame the study as a pilot
ablation rather than definitive evidence of hybrid superiority.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:rw}

\subsection{Hybrid Quantum-Classical Classification}
Hybrid quantum-classical models couple classical feature extractors with shallow PQCs to keep the
quantum component small enough for near-term simulation or
deployment~\cite{cerezo2021vqa,schuld2021machine}. A central design question is where to insert the
quantum block: before, within, or after the classical network. Prior work has explored this idea for
image, text, and tabular settings, but biomedical tabular benchmarks remain comparatively underused
despite their natural low-dimensional structure after feature selection or
PCA~\cite{sagingalieva2023hyperparameter}. Shallow ansatze such as \texttt{BasicEntanglerLayers}
trade expressibility for trainability, reducing the risk of barren plateaus that plague deeper
circuits~\cite{mcclean2018barren}.
Recent work on variational quantum classifiers has demonstrated competitive performance on small
tabular benchmarks when paired with careful data encoding and feature
compression~\cite{jerbi2023quantum,lloyd2024quantumforc},
and quantum kernel methods have shown promise on biomedical classification tasks where
classical kernels struggle with structured feature
interactions~\cite{herrmann2023quantum,canatar2024quantum}.

\subsection{Knowledge Distillation for Quantum Models}
KD transfers information from a stronger teacher to a smaller student through softened targets and has
become a standard compression tool in classical deep learning~\cite{kd}. The distillation loss
interpolates between hard-label cross-entropy and a KL-divergence soft-target term weighted by
temperature $T$ and mixing coefficient $\alpha$. Recent QML work has extended KD to
classical-to-quantum and quantum-to-quantum transfer settings, arguing that a stable classical
teacher can regularize difficult hybrid optimization~\cite{nguyen2022classicalquantum}.
Subsequent studies have shown that KD can improve trainability of variational quantum circuits
by smoothing the loss landscape~\cite{chen2024quantumkd} and that quantum-aware distillation
strategies outperform naive transfer on noisy intermediate-scale
devices~\cite{hur2025quantumknowledge}.
A largely open question is how the information content of teacher soft outputs---measured by their
predictive entropy---affects the magnitude of KD benefit, particularly for small biomedical tasks
where the teacher may already be near-saturated.

\subsection{Biomedical Tabular Classification}
Biomedical tabular tasks typically involve limited sample size, moderate class imbalance, and a need
for interpretable, compact models. Classical baselines such as gradient-boosted trees and small MLPs
are already strong on these benchmarks, raising the bar for any quantum enhancement. PCA-based
compression is especially relevant when quantum inputs must be bounded by the number of available
qubits~\cite{sagingalieva2023hyperparameter}. This makes datasets like WDBC, Pima Indians Diabetes,
and Heart Statlog suitable benchmarks for controlled HQC experiments: they span a range of sample
sizes, feature dimensions, and task difficulties, enabling cross-dataset analysis of how teacher
confidence and KD interact with quantum-layer placement.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem Setup}
Given a binary biomedical tabular dataset $\mathcal{D}=\{(\mathbf{x}_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$ with
$y_i \in \{0,1\}$ and $\mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$, we aim to learn a compact classifier that
keeps predictive quality high while reducing model size and simulation cost.

\subsection{Preprocessing}
Within each training fold, we apply standard scaling followed by PCA to a target dimension
$d' \in \{4,6,8\}$. All preprocessing is fit on the training split only and then applied to the
validation split to avoid data leakage. The default experiments use $d'=4$. Although the codebase
supports SMOTE-based resampling, all reported results here use no class rebalancing.

\subsection{Teacher and Student Models}
The teacher is a classical multilayer perceptron with hidden dimensions $[64,32]$ and dropout $0.2$.
The classical student uses hidden dimensions $[16,8]$.

The hybrid student contains three stages:
\begin{enumerate}
\item a classical projection network,
\item a quantum layer with 4 qubits and 1 variational layer,
\item a classical output head producing a binary logit.
\end{enumerate}
We evaluate three insertion positions for the quantum block: front, middle, and tail. In the front
variant, the first four PCA components are squashed by \texttt{Tanh} and fed directly to the quantum
layer, so this setting has no learnable pre-quantum projection. In the middle and tail variants, a
classical projection network learns a bounded 4-dimensional representation before angle encoding.

\subsection{Quantum Layer and Circuit Design}
\label{subsec:circuit}

The quantum block uses PennyLane's \texttt{AngleEmbedding} with $Y$ rotations for data encoding,
followed by \texttt{BasicEntanglerLayers} for variational processing~\cite{pennylane}. The circuit
for $n=4$ qubits and $L=1$ variational layer is structured as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Encoding:} Apply $R_Y(x_i)$ on qubit $i$ for $i \in \{0,1,2,3\}$.
\item \textbf{Variational layer:} Apply $R_X(\theta_i)$ on each qubit, then a wrap-around ring of
CNOT gates.
\item \textbf{Measurement:} Return $\langle Z_i \rangle$ for all $i$.
\end{enumerate}

A schematic representation using \texttt{quantikz} notation is:
\[
\begin{quantikz}
\lstick{$|0\rangle$} & \gate{R_Y(x_0)} & \gate{R_X(\theta_0)} & \ctrl{1}  & \qw       & \qw       & \targ{}   & \meter{Z} \\
\lstick{$|0\rangle$} & \gate{R_Y(x_1)} & \gate{R_X(\theta_1)} & \targ{}   & \ctrl{1}  & \qw       & \qw       & \meter{Z} \\
\lstick{$|0\rangle$} & \gate{R_Y(x_2)} & \gate{R_X(\theta_2)} & \qw       & \targ{}   & \ctrl{1}  & \qw       & \meter{Z} \\
\lstick{$|0\rangle$} & \gate{R_Y(x_3)} & \gate{R_X(\theta_3)} & \qw       & \qw       & \targ{}   & \ctrl{-3} & \meter{Z}
\end{quantikz}
\]

The effective circuit depth is 2 (encoding + one entangling block), giving $4$ trainable parameters.
This is a deliberately shallow ansatz: it is sufficient to introduce nonlinear quantum correlations
without incurring excessive simulation cost or barren plateau risk~\cite{mcclean2018barren}.

\paragraph{Expressibility.}
For a 4-qubit, 1-layer \texttt{BasicEntanglerLayers} ansatz, the expressibility---measured as the
KL divergence between the distribution of state fidelities and the Haar-random
distribution~\cite{sim2019expressibility}---is relatively low compared to deeper alternating-layered
ansatze. This is a deliberate trade-off: higher expressibility circuits improve representational
capacity but increase gradient variance and simulation cost. For a near-saturated binary task like
WDBC, the shallow ansatz is both practical and sufficient to test placement effects. As discussed
later in Section~\ref{sec:param_efficiency}, the quantum block contributes only four trainable
parameters, so any performance differences cannot be attributed to parameter count alone.

\subsection{Knowledge Distillation}
The student objective combines a hard-label BCE term with a soft-target distillation term:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = (1-\alpha)\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{hard}} + \alpha T^2 \mathcal{L}_{\text{KD}},
\end{equation}
where $T$ is the distillation temperature, $\alpha \in (0,1)$ is the KD weight, and
$\mathcal{L}_{\text{KD}}$ is the KL divergence between teacher and student soft outputs scaled by
$T^2$. In the main runs we use $T=2$ and $\alpha=0.5$.

\subsection{Optimization and Evaluation Protocol}
Unless noted otherwise, all models are trained with Adam at learning rate $10^{-3}$ for 80 epochs.
The default batch size is 32. For the hybrid follow-up runs on Pima and Heart we reduce the batch
size to 8 to keep statevector simulation practical. We do not perform dataset-specific hyperparameter
search; instead, we keep the same optimizer, temperature, and architecture settings across the
reported comparisons. Thresholded metrics use a fixed decision threshold of 0.5.

The main WDBC classical-versus-hybrid comparison is re-run with stratified 10-fold cross-validation.
The WDBC placement, PCA-dimension, and $\alpha$ ablations retain the original 5-fold protocol to stay
comparable with the earlier pilot runs. The companion Pima and Heart benchmark tables use 5-fold
cross-validation, while the follow-up placement checks on those two datasets are reported as 3-fold
pilot experiments because each placement requires a full hybrid retraining loop. We report
mean$\pm$std together with 95\% confidence intervals and paired $t$-tests plus Cohen's $d_z$ effect
sizes for key AUC comparisons.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:disc}

\subsection{Does KD help hybrid students?}
KD helps the hybrid student selectively rather than uniformly. As summarized in
Tables~\ref{tab:stats} and~\ref{tab:cross}, WDBC shows at most a suggestive AUC recovery under KD,
whereas Heart and Pima show modest but still statistically uncertain gains. The pattern is
non-monotonic in teacher entropy and too underpowered to justify a simple ``KD always helps hybrid
models'' claim.

\subsection{Which quantum layer position is best?}
The placement ablation supports a narrower and more implementation-aware conclusion than the
original title suggested. The front variant is fragile because it combines direct PCA-to-qubit
mapping, heavy \texttt{Tanh} saturation, and the removal of a learnable pre-quantum projection.
Middle and tail placement consistently avoid this failure mode, but no universal winner emerges
between them: tail is slightly stronger on WDBC and in Pima AUC, whereas middle is stronger in the
Heart pilot and in Pima's thresholded metrics. The actionable lesson is therefore not ``placement
always dominates,'' but rather ``front placement requires much better input conditioning than this
lightweight pipeline currently provides.''
In short, placement is not a silver bullet; conditioning and projection design are the real levers.

\subsection{Is the hybrid student worth the cost?}
For all three datasets considered here, the answer is still generally no. In the serial 5-fold runs,
classical training remains sub-second per fold, whereas hybrid training requires hundreds of seconds
per fold: roughly 276--330~s on WDBC, 214--221~s on Heart, and 607--617~s on Pima. Inference for
the hybrid models is likewise two to three orders of magnitude slower than the classical students in
statevector simulation. Even when the hybrid models recover some AUC or MCC, those gains are too
small and too statistically fragile to offset the simulation-cost gap. The hybrid layer therefore
behaves more like a research probe than a cost-effective production choice on these small tabular
benchmarks.

\subsection{Parameter Efficiency}
\label{sec:param_efficiency}

Table~\ref{tab:params} compares trainable parameter counts across all models for PCA $d'=4$.
The middle-placement hybrid student uses only 201 parameters (4 quantum, 197 classical), which is
fewer than the classical student's 225, yet achieves comparable AUC and F1 on WDBC.
The front-placement variant is the most compact (53 parameters), but it also removes the learnable
pre-quantum projection used by the middle and tail variants. The tail-placement hybrid (305
parameters) trades a modest parameter increase for the strongest WDBC AUC/MCC operating point. The
parameter table is therefore useful for transparency, but it also shows why the placement ablation
should not be interpreted as a pure ``same-capacity, different-location'' comparison.

\subsection{Limitations}
This work should be read as a pilot ablation study, not as definitive evidence for hybrid quantum
advantage on biomedical tabular tasks. Even after the 10-fold WDBC rerun, several paired confidence
intervals still overlap zero, and the companion Pima/Heart placement checks are only 3-fold pilot
experiments. The datasets themselves remain small (WDBC 569, Heart 270, Pima 768), so broader
generalization is still limited. On the modeling side, we evaluate only one 4-qubit, 1-layer
\texttt{BasicEntanglerLayers} ansatz; we do not report a deeper-circuit comparison, learning-curve
analysis, or an exhaustive hyperparameter search. On the systems side, all experiments use
noiseless simulation on PennyLane's \texttt{default.qubit}~\cite{pennylane}; we do not evaluate
finite-shot sampling, hardware noise, calibration drift, queueing latency, or real NISQ execution.
Future work should extend the same protocol to broader datasets, deeper circuits, and noise-aware
simulation or hardware settings.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conc}

We presented a pilot ablation study of a lightweight hybrid quantum-classical pipeline for
biomedical tabular classification. The updated WDBC 10-fold results do not support a definitive
hybrid advantage over a compact classical baseline: the best hybrid variant remains statistically
indistinguishable from the classical student on the primary AUC comparison. KD can recover some AUC
for the hybrid model, especially away from the near-saturated WDBC regime, but the effect remains
small and statistically uncertain across WDBC, Pima, and Heart.

The placement result also requires a narrower interpretation than the original title implied. In
this implementation, front placement is fragile because it relies on a direct, heavily saturated
PCA-to-qubit mapping with no learnable pre-quantum projection. Middle and tail placement define the
viable region, but their ordering is dataset-dependent rather than universal. The broader practical
conclusion is therefore conservative: strong classical baselines remain hard to beat on small
biomedical tabular benchmarks, and the hybrid simulation cost stays far above the classical
alternatives.

Future work should expand to additional datasets, evaluate noise-aware and shot-based settings, and
test whether the same placement and KD dynamics persist under deeper or more expressive ansatz
families.

\section{Teacher Soft-Output Entropy Analysis}
\label{app:entropy}

To contextualize the limited KD benefit, we analyze the entropy of the teacher's soft predictions
over the training folds. For a binary classifier, the per-sample soft entropy is
\begin{equation}
H(p) = -p \log_2 p - (1-p) \log_2 (1-p),
\end{equation}
where $p = \sigma(z_{\text{teacher}})$ is the teacher's predicted probability. High-confidence
predictions ($p \approx 0$ or $p \approx 1$) yield $H \approx 0$, leaving no additional
information beyond the hard label.

Across all 10 folds and training samples, the teacher's mean soft-output entropy is
$\bar{H}_{\text{train}} = 0.0990$ bits (std.\ $0.2172$), while the validation-fold mean is
$\bar{H}_{\text{val}} = 0.0992$ bits (std.\ $0.2169$). The fraction of high-confidence
predictions satisfying $|p - 0.5| > 0.45$ is $89.7\%$ on training samples and $89.3\%$ on
validation samples. This confirms that the teacher is highly confident on most samples,
and its soft targets provide only limited additional signal relative to one-hot labels.
This helps explain why KD offers limited benefit to the classical student and why
larger $\alpha$ (more soft-target weight) can degrade hybrid student F1/MCC.

The Pima example paints a different KD picture from WDBC. The teacher's mean soft-output entropy is
$0.704$ bits on training folds and $0.707$ bits on validation folds, with only about 14\% of
predictions satisfying $|p-0.5|>0.45$. This indicates substantially richer soft targets than on
WDBC. Correspondingly, the KD hybrid achieves the best AUC and MCC on Pima, while the non-distilled
hybrid retains the best F1. Even so, the practical conclusion remains similar: the hybrid models are
roughly three orders of magnitude slower to train than the classical baselines, so the metric gains
are modest relative to simulation cost. We therefore view Pima as supportive evidence that KD can
matter more when teacher entropy is higher, but not yet enough to overturn the overall cost-benefit
assessment of lightweight classical models.

Heart Statlog lands between WDBC and Pima in teacher uncertainty. The teacher's mean soft-output
entropy is $0.471$ bits on training folds and $0.470$ bits on validation folds, with high-confidence

hybrid improves AUC from $0.8736$ to $0.8857$, MCC from $0.6116$ to $0.6340$, and slightly reduces
training time, but it still does not surpass the classical student on thresholded metrics. The
Heart example therefore supports a narrower version of the Pima conclusion: richer teacher
uncertainty can make KD more useful for the hybrid model, yet the overall cost-benefit picture
still favors lightweight classical baselines.
