\begin{abstract}
Hybrid quantum-classical architectures are easy to overclaim on small biomedical benchmarks, where
strong classical baselines and low statistical power can make minor differences look decisive. We
revisit a lightweight teacher-student pipeline with PCA compression, a compact classical student, a
4-qubit parameterized quantum layer, and knowledge distillation (KD) for biomedical tabular
classification. We rerun the main Breast Cancer Wisconsin Diagnostic (WDBC) comparison with
10-fold cross-validation, retain the original WDBC ablations, and add companion Pima Diabetes and
Heart Statlog benchmarks plus companion 5-fold cross-dataset placement checks.

On WDBC, the tail-placement Hybrid-KD model matches but does not reliably outperform the classical
student (AUC $0.9926 \pm 0.0095$ vs.\ $0.9947 \pm 0.0081$), and the hybrid cost remains in the
hundreds of seconds per fold rather than sub-second. The placement result also needs a narrower
interpretation than our original draft: front insertion is fragile in this implementation because
direct PCA-to-qubit mapping induces heavy \texttt{Tanh} saturation and removes the learnable
pre-quantum projection, whereas middle and tail remain viable. Pima and Heart show only modest,
statistically uncertain hybrid gains under much higher simulation cost.

We therefore present the study as a pilot ablation of placement and input conditioning, not as
definitive evidence that lightweight hybrid models outperform compact classical baselines.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Quantum machine learning (QML) continues to attract interest as a possible route toward practical
learning systems on near-term quantum hardware, following broader discussions of QML and the noisy
intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) regime~\cite{biamonte2017qml,preskill2018nisq,cerezo2021vqa,schuld2021machine}.
In practice, however, large quantum circuits remain hard to optimize and expensive to simulate,
especially in data regimes where strong classical baselines are already available. Hybrid
quantum-classical (HQC) models therefore remain the most realistic design pattern: a classical
front-end performs feature compression, while a small parameterized quantum circuit (PQC) acts as a
learnable nonlinear block~\cite{cerezo2021vqa,benedetti2019pqc}.

Biomedical tabular classification is a useful setting for testing this idea. Such datasets are often
small, feature-limited after preprocessing, and measured under strict performance-cost constraints.
A compact HQC model may therefore provide insight into how and where a quantum block can be inserted,
even if no quantum advantage is expected. At the same time, knowledge distillation (KD) offers a
natural mechanism for transferring supervision from a larger classical teacher to a smaller classical
or hybrid student~\cite{kd}. Recent biomedical and digital-health reviews also note that many QML
claims in healthcare still come from simulator-based studies on small datasets with uneven classical
baselines, making conservative benchmark design essential~\cite{maheshwari2022biomedical,gupta2025digitalhealth}.

This paper asks three concrete questions on the WDBC benchmark:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Does KD help a hybrid quantum-classical student, and how does the KD weight $\alpha$ affect the
trade-off between AUC and F1/MCC?
\item Where should the quantum layer be placed inside the student network?
\item Is the hybrid student worth its simulation cost relative to a lightweight classical student,
considering both training and inference time?
\end{enumerate}

Our contributions are as follows. First, we revisit the main classical-versus-hybrid comparison on
WDBC with a 10-fold robustness check and explicit uncertainty reporting plus fold-budget power
calibration rather than relying only on 5-fold mean$\pm$std summaries. Second, we retain the WDBC
ablations on placement, PCA dimension, and KD weight $\alpha$ while extending the study with
companion Pima Diabetes and Heart Statlog
benchmarks. Third, we add companion 5-fold cross-dataset placement checks on Heart and Pima to test
whether the front/middle/tail pattern is specific to WDBC. Fourth, we provide a circuit diagram, discuss the
limited expressibility of the 4-qubit 1-layer ansatz, and explicitly frame the study as a pilot
ablation rather than definitive evidence of hybrid superiority. Fifth, we connect the empirical
results to recent literature on data encoding, trainability, generalization, and healthcare-oriented
QML benchmarking so that the scope of our claims is explicit.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:rw}

\subsection{Hybrid Quantum-Classical Classification}
Hybrid quantum-classical models couple classical feature extractors with shallow PQCs to keep the
quantum component small enough for near-term simulation or
deployment~\cite{preskill2018nisq,benedetti2019pqc,cerezo2021vqa}. A central design question is not
only where to insert the quantum block, but also how to encode classical features into a limited
quantum register. Circuit-centric classifiers, feature-Hilbert-space formulations, and
quantum-enhanced kernel methods all emphasize that the inductive bias of the model is strongly shaped
by the encoding map, not only by the variational circuit that follows
it~\cite{schuld2020circuit,schuld2019feature,havlicek2019feature}.

Recent work has therefore focused on repeated embeddings and more robust encodings as practical ways
to enlarge function classes without immediately increasing qubit
count~\cite{perezsalinas2020reuploading,schuld2021encoding,larose2020robust}. At the same time,
empirical studies suggest that raw expressibility is not a sufficient design objective: once
trainability and hardware limits are considered, classification accuracy correlates only imperfectly
with expressibility or entangling capability~\cite{hubregtsen2021evaluation,abbas2021power,peters2021highdim}.
Shallow ansatze such as \texttt{BasicEntanglerLayers} therefore remain attractive precisely because
they trade representational headroom for better optimization behavior, especially when deeper
circuits risk barren plateaus~\cite{mcclean2018barren,cerezo2021cost,holmes2022expressibility}.

\subsection{Knowledge Distillation for Quantum Models}
KD transfers information from a stronger teacher to a smaller student through softened targets and has
become a standard compression tool in classical deep learning~\cite{kd}. The distillation loss
interpolates between hard-label cross-entropy and a KL-divergence soft-target term weighted by
temperature $T$ and mixing coefficient $\alpha$. In QML, however, the KD literature is still sparse
compared with classical model compression. Recent surveys and deployment-oriented studies suggest that
teacher-generated soft targets can regularize difficult hybrid optimization and improve robustness of
small quantum or hybrid students, especially when the quantum component is resource
constrained~\cite{chen2024quantumkd,hur2025quantumknowledge}. A largely open question is how the
information content of teacher soft outputs---measured by their predictive entropy---affects the
magnitude of KD benefit, particularly for small biomedical tasks where the teacher may already be
near-saturated.

\subsection{Biomedical Tabular Classification}
Biomedical tabular tasks typically involve limited sample size, moderate class imbalance, and a need
for interpretable, compact models. Classical baselines such as gradient-boosted trees and small MLPs
are already strong on these benchmarks, raising the bar for any quantum enhancement. Recent reviews
of biomedical and healthcare QML also report that many positive findings remain tied to small
datasets, simulator-based evaluations, and heterogeneous baseline choices, so benchmark discipline is
part of the scientific contribution rather than an implementation detail~\cite{maheshwari2022biomedical,gupta2025digitalhealth}.
PCA-based compression is especially relevant when quantum inputs must be bounded by the number of
available qubits~\cite{sagingalieva2023hyperparameter}. This makes datasets like WDBC, Pima Indians
Diabetes, and Heart Statlog suitable benchmarks for controlled HQC experiments: they span a range of
sample sizes, feature dimensions, and task difficulties, enabling cross-dataset analysis of how
teacher confidence and KD interact with quantum-layer placement. More broadly, recent
learning-theoretic work suggests that small-data QML claims should be interpreted through
generalization behavior and uncertainty, not accuracy alone~\cite{caro2022generalization}.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem Setup}
Given a binary biomedical tabular dataset $\mathcal{D}=\{(\mathbf{x}_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$ with
$y_i \in \{0,1\}$ and $\mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$, we aim to learn a compact classifier that
keeps predictive quality high while reducing model size and simulation cost.

\subsection{Preprocessing}
Within each training fold, we apply standard scaling followed by PCA to a target dimension
$d' \in \{4,6,8\}$. All preprocessing is fit on the training split only and then applied to the
validation split to avoid data leakage. The default experiments use $d'=4$. Although the codebase
supports SMOTE-based resampling, all reported results here use no class rebalancing.

The choice of PCA is not only computational convenience. For circuit-centric and angle-encoding-based
quantum classifiers, the feature interface to the quantum register is itself part of the model class,
and richer decision functions usually require either a larger register or repeated data
re-uploading~\cite{schuld2020circuit,perezsalinas2020reuploading,schuld2021encoding}. We therefore
treat PCA as a pragmatic classical-to-quantum interface that compresses tabular structure into a
fixed 4-qubit budget, rather than as a claim that PCA is the uniquely best feature map.

\subsection{Teacher and Student Models}
The teacher is a classical multilayer perceptron with hidden dimensions $[64,32]$ and dropout $0.2$.
The classical student uses hidden dimensions $[16,8]$.

The hybrid student contains three stages:
\begin{enumerate}
\item a classical projection network,
\item a quantum layer with 4 qubits and 1 variational layer,
\item a classical output head producing a binary logit.
\end{enumerate}
We evaluate three insertion positions for the quantum block: front, middle, and tail. In the front
variant, the first four PCA components are squashed by \texttt{Tanh} and fed directly to the quantum
layer, so this setting has no learnable pre-quantum projection. In the middle and tail variants, a
classical projection network learns a bounded 4-dimensional representation before angle encoding.

\subsection{Quantum Layer and Circuit Design}
\label{subsec:circuit}

The quantum block uses PennyLane's \texttt{AngleEmbedding} with $Y$ rotations for data encoding,
followed by \texttt{BasicEntanglerLayers} for variational processing~\cite{pennylane}. The circuit
for $n=4$ qubits and $L=1$ variational layer is structured as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Encoding:} Apply $R_Y(x_i)$ on qubit $i$ for $i \in \{0,1,2,3\}$.
\item \textbf{Variational layer:} Apply $R_X(\theta_i)$ on each qubit, then a wrap-around ring of
CNOT gates.
\item \textbf{Measurement:} Return $\langle Z_i \rangle$ for all $i$.
\end{enumerate}

A schematic representation using \texttt{quantikz} notation is:
\[
\begin{quantikz}
\lstick{$|0\rangle$} & \gate{R_Y(x_0)} & \gate{R_X(\theta_0)} & \ctrl{1}  & \qw       & \qw       & \targ{}   & \meter{Z} \\
\lstick{$|0\rangle$} & \gate{R_Y(x_1)} & \gate{R_X(\theta_1)} & \targ{}   & \ctrl{1}  & \qw       & \qw       & \meter{Z} \\
\lstick{$|0\rangle$} & \gate{R_Y(x_2)} & \gate{R_X(\theta_2)} & \qw       & \targ{}   & \ctrl{1}  & \qw       & \meter{Z} \\
\lstick{$|0\rangle$} & \gate{R_Y(x_3)} & \gate{R_X(\theta_3)} & \qw       & \qw       & \targ{}   & \ctrl{-3} & \meter{Z}
\end{quantikz}
\]

The effective circuit depth is 2 (encoding + one entangling block), giving $4$ trainable parameters.
This is a deliberately shallow ansatz: it is sufficient to introduce nonlinear quantum correlations
without incurring excessive simulation cost or barren plateau risk~\cite{mcclean2018barren}.
Angle embedding is chosen because it is simple, differentiable, and easy to match to bounded PCA
coordinates, but it also constrains the accessible frequency spectrum unless the encoding is repeated
or supported by a richer pre-quantum projection~\cite{perezsalinas2020reuploading,larose2020robust,schuld2021encoding}.

\paragraph{Expressibility.}
For a 4-qubit, 1-layer \texttt{BasicEntanglerLayers} ansatz, the expressibility---measured as the
KL divergence between the distribution of state fidelities and the Haar-random
distribution~\cite{sim2019expressibility}---is relatively low compared to deeper alternating-layered
ansatze. This is a deliberate trade-off: higher expressibility can improve representational
capacity, but empirical and theoretical work suggests that classification quality depends on how
expressibility interacts with gradient scale, cost locality, and encoding choice rather than on
expressibility alone~\cite{hubregtsen2021evaluation,abbas2021power,holmes2022expressibility}. We
therefore favor a shallow circuit and local-output training objective, which aligns with trainability
guidance from barren plateau analyses~\cite{mcclean2018barren,cerezo2021cost}. For a near-saturated
binary task like WDBC, the shallow ansatz is both practical and sufficient to test placement
effects. As discussed later in Section~\ref{sec:param_efficiency}, the quantum block contributes only
four trainable parameters, so any performance differences cannot be attributed to parameter count
alone.

\subsection{Knowledge Distillation}
The student objective combines a hard-label BCE term with a soft-target distillation term:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = (1-\alpha)\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{hard}} + \alpha T^2 \mathcal{L}_{\text{KD}},
\end{equation}
where $T$ is the distillation temperature, $\alpha \in (0,1)$ is the KD weight, and
$\mathcal{L}_{\text{KD}}$ is the KL divergence between teacher and student soft outputs scaled by
$T^2$. In the main runs we use $T=2$ and $\alpha=0.5$.

\subsection{Optimization and Evaluation Protocol}
Unless noted otherwise, all models are trained with Adam at learning rate $10^{-3}$ for 80 epochs.
The default batch size is 32. For the hybrid follow-up runs on Pima and Heart we reduce the batch
size to 8 to keep statevector simulation practical. We do not perform dataset-specific hyperparameter
search; instead, we keep the same optimizer, temperature, and architecture settings across the
reported comparisons. Thresholded metrics use a fixed decision threshold of 0.5.

The main WDBC classical-versus-hybrid comparison is re-run with stratified 10-fold cross-validation.
The WDBC placement, PCA-dimension, and $\alpha$ ablations retain the original 5-fold protocol to stay
comparable with the earlier pilot runs. The companion Pima and Heart benchmark tables use 5-fold
cross-validation, and the follow-up placement checks on those two datasets are now rerun with the
same 5-fold budget because the earlier 3-fold pilot left too much uncertainty around the
middle-versus-tail ordering. We report
mean$\pm$std together with 95\% confidence intervals and paired $t$-tests plus Cohen's $d_z$ effect
sizes for key AUC comparisons. To calibrate how much evidence each fold budget can carry, we also use
a simple paired-test sensitivity benchmark: at $\alpha=0.05$, reaching 80\% power would require
roughly $d_z \approx 1.68$ and $1.00$ for 5- and 10-fold comparisons, respectively.

The WDBC placement study in Table~\ref{tab:ablation_pos} still reveals a sharp front-versus-bounded
gap, but the mechanism needs a narrower interpretation than in the original draft. In the front

front placement is therefore an input-conditioning failure mode rather than evidence that front
insertion is fundamentally impossible.

Middle and tail placement define the viable operating region. On WDBC, tail gives the best AUC/MCC
while middle gives the best F1. On the new Heart companion 5-fold check, front collapses again

$d_z=0.65$ on Pima). We therefore read the 5-fold companion checks as strong evidence against front
placement in this implementation, but still not as a decisive ranking between middle and tail.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:disc}

\subsection{Does KD help hybrid students?}
KD helps the hybrid student selectively rather than uniformly. As summarized in
Tables~\ref{tab:stats} and~\ref{tab:cross}, WDBC shows at most a suggestive AUC recovery under KD,
whereas Heart and Pima show modest but still statistically uncertain gains. The pattern is
non-monotonic in teacher entropy and too underpowered to justify a simple ``KD always helps hybrid
models'' claim. One useful interpretation is through the soft-target signal itself: the KD gradient
is driven by the gap between student and teacher softened probabilities, so highly confident
teachers contribute little beyond the hard labels. On WDBC, about 89.3\% of validation predictions
satisfy $|p-0.5|>0.45$, leaving little extra soft information; Heart and Pima reduce that
high-confidence fraction to 36.3\% and 14.2\%, respectively, which makes KD more capable of
reshaping hybrid updates. Even there, however, the realized benefit still depends on teacher quality
and fold budget, which is why Heart improves more than Pima despite the latter's higher entropy.

\subsection{Which quantum layer position is best?}
The placement ablation supports a narrower and more implementation-aware conclusion than the
original title suggested. The front variant is fragile because it combines direct PCA-to-qubit
mapping, heavy \texttt{Tanh} saturation, and the removal of a learnable pre-quantum projection.
Middle and tail placement consistently avoid this failure mode, but no universal winner emerges
between them: tail is slightly stronger on WDBC and in Pima AUC, whereas Heart is effectively tied
in AUC and middle keeps a small edge on thresholded metrics in both Heart and Pima. The actionable lesson is therefore not ``placement
always dominates,'' but rather ``front placement requires much better input conditioning than this
lightweight pipeline currently provides.''
In short, placement is not a silver bullet; conditioning and projection design are the real levers.
A deeper ansatz or repeated data re-uploading could alter this picture by re-injecting nonlinear
mixing after successive embeddings and reducing the dependence on one saturated front projection. We
do not test that regime here, so the front-placement failure should be interpreted as specific to the
present 4-qubit, 1-layer \texttt{BasicEntanglerLayers} pipeline, not as an ansatz-independent
impossibility claim.

\subsection{Is the hybrid student worth the cost?}
For all three datasets considered here, the answer is still generally no. In the serial 5-fold runs,
classical training remains sub-second per fold, whereas hybrid training requires hundreds of seconds
per fold: roughly 276--330~s on WDBC, 214--221~s on Heart, and 607--617~s on Pima. Inference for
the hybrid models is likewise two to three orders of magnitude slower than the classical students in
statevector simulation. Even when the hybrid models recover some AUC or MCC, those gains are too
small and too statistically fragile to offset the simulation-cost gap. The hybrid layer therefore
behaves more like a research probe than a cost-effective production choice on these small tabular
benchmarks.
Because every metric and timing number here comes from noiseless statevector simulation, this
cost-benefit comparison is still simulator-limited. Real shot-based execution would introduce
sampling variance, hardware noise, queueing, and calibration overhead, so the present results are
better read as an optimistic upper bound on attainable quantum utility than as a hardware-realistic
deployment estimate.

\subsection{Benchmark Validity and External Plausibility}
Recent QML theory and benchmarking work cautions that small accuracy deltas on classical data can be
driven as much by encoding choice, sample size, and estimator noise as by genuinely useful quantum
effects~\cite{caro2022generalization,peters2021highdim,thanasilp2024kernel}. Our results fit that
pattern. The 4-qubit block can match compact classical baselines on some folds, but the evidence does
not isolate a uniquely quantum benefit: the best gains appear only after aggressive classical
preprocessing, remain statistically fragile, and weaken once cost is treated as part of the
comparison.

This conservative reading is especially important in healthcare-facing QML. Systematic reviews of the
biomedical literature report that most studies remain simulator-based, use small or repeatedly reused
benchmark datasets, and rarely establish stable superiority over carefully tuned classical
baselines~\cite{maheshwari2022biomedical,gupta2025digitalhealth}. The present study is therefore best
understood as a controlled measurement of placement and conditioning effects inside a hybrid
pipeline under simulator conditions, not as a translational claim about clinical deployment or
NISQ-ready advantage.

\subsection{Limitations}
This work should be read as a pilot ablation study, not as definitive evidence for hybrid quantum
advantage on biomedical tabular tasks. Even after the 10-fold WDBC rerun, several paired confidence
intervals still overlap zero; the observed Table~\ref{tab:stats} effects correspond to achieved
powers of only 0.43--0.51 for the 10-fold WDBC contrasts and 0.11--0.17 for the 5-fold Pima/Heart
KD contrasts. The companion Pima/Heart placement checks now use five folds, which removes the
earlier 3-fold pilot bottleneck but still leaves limited sensitivity for subtle ordering claims: a
two-sided paired test at $n=5$ needs about $d_z \approx 1.68$ for 80\% power. In practice, that is
enough to resolve front-versus-bounded contrasts ($d_z=3.29$--$6.30$) but not middle-versus-tail
differences ($d_z=0.02$ on Heart and $0.65$ on Pima). The datasets themselves remain small (WDBC
569, Heart 270, Pima 768), so broader generalization is still limited. On the modeling side, we evaluate only one
4-qubit, 1-layer
\texttt{BasicEntanglerLayers} ansatz; we do not report a deeper-circuit comparison, learning-curve
analysis, or an exhaustive hyperparameter search, nor do we test repeated data re-uploading or more
robust encodings that could alter the front-placement
story~\cite{perezsalinas2020reuploading,larose2020robust,schuld2021encoding}. On the systems side,
all experiments use noiseless simulation on PennyLane's \texttt{default.qubit}~\cite{pennylane}; the
current claims are therefore simulator-limited and should be read as idealized upper bounds rather
than hardware-realistic NISQ evidence. We do not evaluate finite-shot sampling, hardware noise,
calibration drift, queueing latency, or real NISQ execution. That omission matters because recent
work shows that kernel concentration and shot noise can erase nominal gains once hardware
constraints are part of the evaluation~\cite{peters2021highdim,thanasilp2024kernel}. A deeper or
re-uploading circuit might reduce the current front-insertion failure mode, but it would also change
the cost/trainability envelope of the study.
Future work should extend the same protocol to broader datasets, deeper circuits, and noise-aware
simulation or hardware settings.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conc}

We presented a pilot ablation study of a lightweight hybrid quantum-classical pipeline for
biomedical tabular classification. The updated WDBC 10-fold results do not support a definitive
hybrid advantage over a compact classical baseline: the best hybrid variant remains statistically
indistinguishable from the classical student on the primary AUC comparison. KD can recover some AUC
for the hybrid model, especially away from the near-saturated WDBC regime, but the effect remains
small and statistically uncertain across WDBC, Pima, and Heart.

The placement result also requires a narrower interpretation than the original title implied. In
this implementation, front placement is fragile because it relies on a direct, heavily saturated
PCA-to-qubit mapping with no learnable pre-quantum projection. Middle and tail placement define the
viable region, but their ordering is dataset-dependent rather than universal. The broader practical
conclusion is therefore conservative: strong classical baselines remain hard to beat on small
biomedical tabular benchmarks, and the hybrid simulation cost stays far above the classical
alternatives. The new 5-fold Heart/Pima placement reruns strengthen only the bounded part of that
story: they confirm front collapse, but they also show that middle-versus-tail differences remain too
small and unstable to support a universal ordering claim.

Future work should expand to additional datasets, evaluate noise-aware and shot-based settings, and
test whether the same placement and KD dynamics persist under deeper or more expressive ansatz
families. More broadly, the best-supported contribution of the present study is methodological rather
than utility-driven: it clarifies how placement, input conditioning, and supervision transfer
interact inside a lightweight hybrid pipeline under simulator conditions. It does not establish
practical quantum advantage or deployment-ready value on these biomedical tabular benchmarks.

\section{Teacher Soft-Output Entropy Analysis}
\label{app:entropy}

To contextualize the limited KD benefit, we analyze the entropy of the teacher's soft predictions
over the training folds. For a binary classifier, the per-sample soft entropy is
\begin{equation}
H(p) = -p \log_2 p - (1-p) \log_2 (1-p),
\end{equation}
where $p = \sigma(z_{\text{teacher}})$ is the teacher's predicted probability. High-confidence
predictions ($p \approx 0$ or $p \approx 1$) yield $H \approx 0$, leaving no additional
information beyond the hard label. In the KD term, the student-logit gradient is proportional to the
gap between student and teacher softened probabilities, so low-entropy teacher outputs collapse the
distillation signal toward the hard-label BCE gradient.

Across all 10 folds and training samples, the teacher's mean soft-output entropy is
$\bar{H}_{\text{train}} = 0.0990$ bits (std.\ $0.2172$), while the validation-fold mean is
$\bar{H}_{\text{val}} = 0.0992$ bits (std.\ $0.2169$). The fraction of high-confidence
predictions satisfying $|p - 0.5| > 0.45$ is $89.7\%$ on training samples and $89.3\%$ on
validation samples. This confirms that the teacher is highly confident on most samples,
and its soft targets provide only limited additional signal relative to one-hot labels.
This helps explain why KD offers limited benefit to the classical student and why
larger $\alpha$ (more soft-target weight) can degrade hybrid student F1/MCC. The WDBC temperature
sweep in Table~\ref{tab:ablation_pca} is consistent with this reading: raising $T$ from 2 to 4
leaves AUC nearly unchanged but reduces F1/MCC, suggesting that additional softening mainly spreads
probability mass without creating a stronger supervisory ordering signal.
